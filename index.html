Nano Banana 3 vs DALL-E 3: A Comparative Prompt Test Study

BY: JAMARIA BRISBY

Introduction

This research initiative serves as a formal benchmarking study designed to evaluate the current frontiers of Generative Artificial Intelligence, specifically focusing on the performance of Google's Nano Banana 3 and OpenAI’s DALL-E 3. As AI models move toward multimodal capabilities, understanding the relationship between linguistic input and visual output is paramount. This newsletter documents a controlled environment study where both models were subjected to identical "Prompt Engineering" stressors to observe their respective neural logic.

Our primary objective is to analyze Structural Consistency and Latent Space Adherence. In the context of "Phase 2" research, it is no longer sufficient for a model to simply generate a "pretty" image; we are now measuring for "Technical Fidelity." This refers to a model's ability to maintain a persistent architectural environment while incrementally adding complex layers such as lighting vectors, weather effects, and specific camera angles without losing the original structural "DNA" of the scene.

Throughout the following analysis, we examine how Google’s Nano Banana 3 prioritizes cinematic realism and environmental persistence, contrasting it with OpenAI’s DALL-E 3, which tends to prioritize illustrative clarity and object density. By comparing these six distinct generations, we can draw concrete conclusions about which model serves as a superior tool for professional designers requiring precise control over their generated assets.

PROMPT TEST RESEARCH FINDINGS

Test 01: Baseline Structural Foundation
"A futuristic city"
Google: Nano Banana 3
OpenAI: DALL-E 3

Technical Comparison & Benchmarking:

In the baseline execution, we observed a fundamental difference in how these two engines interpret high-level descriptors. Google's Nano Banana 3 established a scene rooted in architectural logic, utilizing 3D depth and realistic vanishing points. The generated buildings feel grounded in a physical space, providing a professional-grade "canvas" for future modifications. This model successfully passed the baseline test by avoiding "hallucinated" geometry.

In contrast, OpenAI's DALL-E 3 executed the prompt with an illustrative bias. While the image is visually striking, it leans heavily toward digital art rather than photorealism. The colors are highly saturated, and the building shapes are more abstract. DALL-E 3 succeeded in capturing the "vibe" of a future city, but failed to provide the structural realism found in the Google output.

Comparing the two, Google's image is a superior starting point for professional rendering. The light source in the Google image is consistent across the entire frame, whereas DALL-E 3 has multiple conflicting light sources that make the city feel like a composite rather than a single environment. This suggests Google's latent space is better at understanding "global illumination" from the start.

Overall, Google executed this better because it focused on the "bones" of the city. OpenAI focused on the "painting" of the city. For a research project centered on technical accuracy, Google's ability to create a realistic 3D space gives it the early lead in this benchmarking study.

Test 02: Layered Environmental Illumination
"A futuristic city at night with neon lights, flying cars, and tall glass skyscrapers"
Google: Nano Banana 3
OpenAI: DALL-E 3

Technical Comparison & Benchmarking:

Test 02 represents a critical turning point in our research: Structural Consistency. Google's Nano Banana 3 executed this perfectly by retaining the same city layout from Test 01. It successfully "updated" the environment to nighttime and added neon elements without changing the buildings. This proves that Google’s model has superior latent memory, allowing for iterative design work.

Conversely, OpenAI's DALL-E 3 failed the consistency check. Instead of taking the city from Test 01 and turning the lights on, it generated a completely different city with a new layout. While the neon colors are vibrant, the inability to maintain the "DNA" of the previous image makes DALL-E 3 difficult to use for projects requiring multiple shots of the same location.

Technically, Google's execution of "Neon Bloom" is more realistic. Notice how the blue and pink light actually reflects off the glass surfaces of the skyscrapers. DALL-E 3’s neon lights look like they are "drawn on" top of the buildings rather than being a physical light source that interacts with the materials of the city.

In conclusion, Google passed this test by showing it can handle complex environmental layering while keeping the scene's identity intact. OpenAI failed by "resetting" the scene. Google’s engine behaves like a lighting technician on a film set, while OpenAI’s engine behaves like an artist starting a brand-new sketch from scratch.

Test 03: Professional Fidelity Stress Test
"A cyberpunk city... rainy streets... 4K, wide angle."
Google: Nano Banana 3
OpenAI: DALL-E 3

Technical Comparison & Benchmarking:

The final stress test evaluated Tag Adherence. Google's Nano Banana 3 dominated this category by being the only model to successfully obey the "wide angle" and "rainy street" instructions. The reflection of the holograms on the wet pavement in the Google image is a masterclass in AI ray-tracing. The model understood that water on a surface creates a mirror effect, executing a "pass" on professional-grade fidelity.

OpenAI's DALL-E 3, while beautiful, failed the "wide angle" requirement. It provided a close-up, cluttered view that ignored the camera perspective requested in the prompt. While DALL-E 3’s holograms are very clear, they feel disconnected from the environment. There is no reflection of that hologram in the puddles on the ground, which is a significant failure of environmental awareness.

The contrast here is between Cinematic Realism (Google) and Visual Clarity (OpenAI). Google's image feels like a still from a high-budget movie. The rain creates a "mood" and a texture that feels tangible. OpenAI's image feels like a high-quality poster; it is very easy to read, but it doesn't feel like a real place you could step into.

Final verdict: Google Nano Banana 3 is the superior tool for technical prompt engineering. It follows camera tags, maintains structural DNA across multiple tests, and understands complex physics like reflections. OpenAI is excellent for quick, vibrant concepts, but lacks the professional control and "memory" required for high-level AI research projects.
