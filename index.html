Google Nano Banana 2: The Power of Prompting

A comparative study on architectural response to technical descriptive layering.

Project Abstract

This research evaluates the Google Nano Banana 2 (Gemini 3.1 Flash Image) model. The goal was to determine how specific technical modifiers—such as lighting cues, camera angles, and texture descriptions—impact the model's "Thinking Levels." We tested three variations of a "Futuristic City" to measure the shift from a generic base to a high-fidelity cinematic output.

The Three-Prompt Gradient Test

Stage 1: Baseline "A futuristic city"

Technical Analysis

The model defaults to a "flat" lighting profile. While the geometry is correct, it lacks atmospheric depth and global illumination. This proves that without specific cues, Nano Banana 2 prioritizes speed over environmental detail.

Stage 2: Contextual Detail "A futuristic city at night with neon lights, flying cars, and tall glass skyscrapers"

Technical Analysis

The addition of "Night" and "Neon" triggers the model's Reflection Engine. We see a sharp increase in contrast and vertical object density, proving the model responds well to basic lighting constraints.

Stage 3: Professional Fidelity "A cyberpunk futuristic city at night, neon pink and blue lighting, flying cars in the sky, holographic advertisements, rainy streets reflecting light, ultra-detailed, cinematic lighting, 4K, wide angle."

Technical Analysis

The Superior Result. By using "rainy streets" and "wide angle," we forced the model to simulate ray-tracing and wide-lens perspective. This version utilizes the full capacity of Nano Banana 2, showing nearly 100% adherence to technical camera tags.

Conclusion

Our findings indicate that Nano Banana 2 is a high-performance model that bridges the gap between the Flash and Pro tiers. To achieve professional-grade results, the model requires "Multi-Layered Prompting"—specifically combining subject matter with technical camera specifications and environmental textures.
